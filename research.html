The Mystery of Disk Accretion

Picture a young star surrounded by a swirling disk of gas and dustâ€”the birthplace of planets. For this star to grow and for planets to form, material needs to spiral inward toward the star in a process called accretion. But there's a problem: the laws of physics say this shouldn't happen easily.

For decades, astronomers believed that turbulence driven by the magnetorotational instability (MRI) was the engine moving material inward. Think of it like a cosmic mixer, stirring the disk and allowing gas to lose angular momentum and fall toward the star. But when scientists looked closerâ€”through observations and detailed computer simulationsâ€”they found a troubling truth: MRI doesn't work well in most of the disk. So if MRI can't do the job, what can? Enter magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) windsâ€” magnetic fields that launch material upward and outward from the disk surface while simultaneously allowing the remaining material to spiral inward. It's a beautiful cosmic paradox: by throwing some material away, the disk can pull the rest inward.

The Final Act: How Disks Die

Protoplanetary disks don't last forever. Within a few million years, the gasâ€”the fuel for giant planet formationâ€”vanishes. Once the gas is gone, no more Jupiters or Saturns can form. Rocky planets can still assemble from leftover dust, but the window for gas giants closes.

Astronomers have long theorized that photoevaporative windsâ€”driven by intense X-rays and extreme ultraviolet (EUV) radiation from the central starâ€”are responsible for clearing the gas. Imagine the star as a cosmic hairdryer, heating the disk surface until gas molecules move fast enough to escape the star's gravity entirely. The gas doesn't fall inward or get ejected by magnetic fieldsâ€”it just... leaves.

This process determines the fate of planetary systems. Clear the gas too early, and you get a system like oursâ€”rocky planets close in, maybe a few gas giants farther out. Clear it too late, and you might end up with hot Jupiters or multiple giant planets. Photoevaporation sets the clock.

But for 30 years, this was all theory. No one had ever directly seen a photoevaporative wind clearing a diskâ€”until my team used JWST to look at T Chamaeleontis.

The First Image: Watching a Disk Evaporate

T Chamaeleontis (T Cha) is what we call a "transitional disk"â€”a disk with a large gap cleared of dust, like a donut. We suspected this gap was carved by a photoevaporative wind, but we needed proof.

Using JWST's MIRI instrument, which observes in the mid-infrared, I led observations targeting noble gas emission linesâ€”specifically neon and argon. These atoms are perfect tracers: they don't stick to dust, they glow brightly when ionized by X-rays and UV light, and their emission patterns tell us exactly where the wind is and what's powering it.

The first direct image of a photoevaporative wind: JWST/MIRI reveals spatially extended [Ne II] emission from T Chaâ€”gas being blown away by the star's high-energy radiation.

What we saw was groundbreaking. The [Ne II] emission was spatially extendedâ€”it wasn't just a point source at the star; it spread out across the disk. This was direct, visual proof that gas was being launched from the disk surface and escaping into space.

We detected four noble gas lines in total: [Ne II], [Ne III], [Ar II], and [Ar III]. The [Ar III] detection was a firstâ€”no one had ever seen that line in any protoplanetary disk before. By comparing the ratios of these lines, we determined that neon is ionized by X-rays, while argon is ionized by EUV photons. The star's high-energy radiation is the engine driving the wind.

A 30-Year Question Answered

This was the first spatially resolved detection of a photoevaporative disk wind. After three decades of theory and indirect evidence, we finally saw it happening. T Cha's disk is being cleared by radiation-driven winds, and we watched it in action.

Understanding the Wind: Models Meet Reality

Seeing the wind was the first step. Understanding its physics required sophisticated modeling. Working with Dr. Andrew Sellek and collaborators, we used hydrodynamic simulations combined with radiative transfer calculations to interpret what we saw.

The models revealed that the wind is denseâ€”much denser than expected, with electron densities around 10,000â€“100,000 particles per cubic centimeter. It launches from about 1 AU from the star and extends outward beyond 10 AU, matching the location of the dust gap.

Hydrodynamic models of photoevaporative winds, combined with radiative transfer and synthetic JWST observations, reproduce the observed noble gas emission and constrain the wind's density and launching region.

The compact [Ar II] emission compared to the extended [Ne II] emission told us something critical: the wind is so dense that it shields itself from soft X-rays, creating distinct ionization zones. Hard X-rays penetrate deep and ionize neon everywhere, but softer photons are absorbed in the wind's outer layers, ionizing argon only in specific regions.

This shielding effect is a hallmark of thermal photoevaporationâ€”the wind is driven by heating, not magnetic fields. In T Cha's outer disk, at least, photoevaporation is the dominant clearing mechanism.

From Detection to Understanding

By combining JWST observations with state-of-the-art models, we didn't just see the windâ€”we measured its density, temperature, ionization state, and launching radius. This is the first detailed physical characterization of a photoevaporative wind, giving us the tools to understand how disk gas is cleared and how that impacts planet formation.

Disks Around Double Stars: A New Frontier

Most stars aren't aloneâ€”many have binary companions, two stars orbiting each other. When a protoplanetary disk surrounds both stars, we call it a circumbinary disk. These systems are fascinating laboratories for planet formation because the gravitational dance of two stars creates unique challenges and opportunities.

The orbiting binary carves a large gap in the inner disk, like a cosmic lawnmower clearing a path. This affects how material accretes onto the stars, how winds are launched, and how planets can form. We've found circumbinary planetsâ€”worlds orbiting both stars, like Tatooine from Star Warsâ€”but we still don't fully understand how they got there.

My work on circumbinary disks is in its early stages, but the questions are compelling: How do MHD winds behave when there are two stars stirring the magnetic field? Does photoevaporation work differently when the radiation comes from two sources? And what does this mean for planet formation in these exotic systems?

Early Results and Future Directions

Using JWST, I'm beginning to observe circumbinary disks with the same techniques we used for single-star systems. The goal is to see if the nested structure of MHD winds and the signatures of photoevaporation appear in these more complex environments.

Preliminary observations suggest that circumbinary disks do launch winds, but the structure may be more complex due to the binary's gravitational perturbations. In some systems, we see asymmetriesâ€”one side of the disk may be brighter or more extended than the other, possibly due to the orbital motion of the stars.
