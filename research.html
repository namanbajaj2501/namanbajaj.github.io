My research focuses on the critical processes that shape protoplanetary disks‚Äîthe birthplaces of planets. Protoplanetary Disk Evolution

How do protoplanetary disks evolve? For decades, astronomers thought turbulence driven by the magnetorotational instability (MRI) was responsible for moving material inward (accretion) in these disks. However, observations and modeling suggest MRI doesn't work efficiently in the cold, weakly-ionized regions of protoplanetary disks. Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) winds‚Äî magnetic fields that launch material outward while simultaneously driving accretion‚Äîhave emerged as a compelling alternative mechanism.

Revealing the Nested Structure of Disk Winds First JWST Detection

Lead: Pascucci et al. (2025)
Journal: Nature Astronomy
Instrument: JWST/NIRSpec
My Role: Key contributor: Performed data calibration
The Big Question

If MHD winds drive disk evolution, what do they look like? Theory predicts a "nested" structure with different wind components at different speeds and temperatures‚Äîbut only a few have been directly observed before.

Using JWST's NIRSpec instrument, we observed four edge-on protoplanetary disks in the Taurus star-forming region with unprecedented spatial resolution. Amazingly, we directly imaged the predicted nested structure of disk winds in all four disks, suggesting that disk winds are common in protoplanetary disks.

JWST/NIRSpec spectro-imaging reveals the nested morphology of disk winds: a fast collimated [Fe II] jet at the core, surrounded by slower H‚ÇÇ emission, with broader CO emission forming the outermost layer towards the edge-on disk of HH 30.

Key Discoveries
  • Layered wind structure: All four disks show a fast, collimated [Fe II] jet at the center, surrounded by a hollow cavity of slower H‚ÇÇ gas, and in one disk, broader CO emission forming an outer layer
  • Chemical stratification: Different molecules trace different wind launching regions and temperatures
  • Wind launch radius Winds in all four protoplanetary disks launch from between 1 and 10 au from the star
  • Direct MHD evidence: The nested morphology along with the derived launch radius strongly supports magnetohydrodynamic winds as the origin of the observed winds

This discovery provides the first direct observational confirmation that MHD winds are operating in protoplanetary disks, fundamentally changing our understanding of how material moves in planet-forming environments.

Protoplanetary Disk Dispersal

The final act of planet formation: how do protoplanetary disks lose their gas? Photoevaporative winds‚Äîdriven by high-energy stellar radiation (X-rays and extreme UV)‚Äîare thought to clear disk gas in just a few million years, essentially stopping giant planet formation and determining which planetary systems can host Jupiters. Despite decades of work, direct observational evidence (spatial) has been elusive‚Äîuntil JWST.

First Spatially Resolved Images of a Photoevaporative Disk Wind First Author Breakthrough Discovery

Lead: Bajaj et al. (2024)
Journal: Astronomical Journal
Instrument: JWST/MIRI-MRS
My Role: First Author
The Challenge

Photoevaporative winds have been theorized for 30+ years, but no telescope before JWST had the sensitivity and spatial resolution in the mid-infrared to actually see them.

Using JWST's MIRI Medium Resolution Spectrograph (MRS), we led observations of T Chamaeleontis (T Cha)‚Äîa transitional disk with a large dust gap. We detected the first spatially extended, directly imaged photoevaporative disk wind through mid-infrared noble gas emission lines.

JWST/MIRI-MRS observations reveal spatially extended [Ne II] 12.81 Œºm emission from the T Cha disk wind‚Äîthe first direct image of a photoevaporative wind clearing a protoplanetary disk.

Key Discoveries
  • First direct detection: Spatially resolved [Ne II] 12.81 Œºm emission‚Äîthe first time a photoevaporative wind has been directly imaged
  • Noble gas detections: Four forbidden noble gas lines detected: [Ne II], [Ne III], [Ar II], and [Ar III]‚Äîwith [Ar III] detected for the first time in any protoplanetary disk
  • Ionization mechanisms: Line ratios reveal that argon is ionized by extreme UV photons while neon is ionized by X-rays, pinpointing the energy sources likely driving the wind
  • Clearing timescales: Measurements constrain how quickly gas is removed, directly impacting when giant planet formation must cease

This discovery represents direct observational proof that photoevaporative winds are actively clearing gas from protoplanetary disks, confirming decades of theoretical predictions and opening a new era in studying disk dispersal.

Modeling the T Cha Disk Wind: Constraining Wind Density and Launch Radius Co-Author

Lead: Sellek et al. (2024)
Journal: Astronomical Journal
Instrument: JWST/MIRI-MRS
My Role: Key Author: Performed Comparisons of models with observations
From Observation to Understanding

Detecting the wind was just the first step. To truly understand how photoevaporation works, we needed sophisticated radiative transfer modeling to decode what the observed emission lines tell us about the wind's physical properties.

In this companion study led by Dr. Andrew Sellek, we used advanced hydrodynamic wind models combined with radiative transfer calculations to interpret the JWST/MIRI-MRS observations of T Cha. The goal: determine the wind's density, temperature structure, and where it's launched from in the disk.

Hydrodynamic wind models combined with radiative transfer calculations and MIRISim reproduce the observed noble gas emission, revealing a dense photoevaporative wind launched from ~1-2 au.

Key Findings
  • Dense wind confirmed: Models require a high-density wind (ne ~ 10‚Å¥‚Äì10‚Åµ cm‚Åª¬≥) to match observed line luminosities
  • Launch radius: Wind originates from ~1 AU but extends to >10 AU, consistent with theoretical photoevaporative wind models
  • X-ray vs. EUV ionization: The compact [Ar II] emission (compared to extended [Ne II]) requires neon ionization by hard X-rays and argon ionization by softer X-rays/EUV photons
  • Shielding effects: The dense wind shields itself from soft X-rays, creating distinct ionization zones that produce the observed noble gas emission pattern
  • Photoevaporation confirmed: The wind properties favor a thermal photoevaporative origin over MHD launching for the outer disk regions

By matching theoretical models to our JWST observations, this work provides the first detailed physical characterization of a photoevaporative wind, including its density structure, ionization state, and launching mechanism‚Äîcritical information for understanding how disk gas dispersal impacts planet formation outcomes.

The Big Picture

My research uses JWST to answer fundamental questions about planet formation: How do disks evolve? How do they disperse? And how do these processes determine which planetary systems can form giant planets like Jupiter?

By directly imaging disk winds‚Äîboth the MHD winds that drive evolution and the photoevaporative winds that clear gas‚Äîwe're witnessing the forces that shaped our own solar system 4.6 billion years ago, now playing out in real-time around young stars across the galaxy.

These discoveries are just the beginning. JWST is opening a new window into planet formation, and the next few years promise even more breakthroughs in understanding how planetary systems‚Äî including habitable worlds‚Äîcome to be. My ongoing projects will expand on this initial results and provide an even broader understanding
